Snowbird

Rake was sweating. That was how he knew he was going to die. From birth, it was a lesson drilled into everyone, every day. Never go overdressed into the cold, and never overexert yourself. If you start sweating, it will freeze your clothes, they’d said. If your clothes freeze, so too will you. And if you freeze, you will die.

And so, Rake was dying. It was the simple, hard, freezing cold truth. He’d been warned, again and again, that to attempt what he had was foolish, childish and stubborn. But the Rite of Age was sacred, as was the right of each Citizen-To-Be to choose their Rite. The rules governing the Rites were vague and amorphous—intentionally so. In order to become a Citizen, a Citizen-To-Be was expected to exercise creativity, judgment and ingenuity. That wasn’t to say that every Rite was a one-of-a-kind journey, entirely unique in and of itself. Nobody would every say it aloud of course, but there were the standard Rites that were perfectly acceptable. Leaving the Community to find a functional piece of technica was considered a prestigious, if uncreative Rite. No one would think less of you for it, but it would win you neither notoriety nor prestige.

Rake, of course, had never even considered a “standard” Rite. For him, forgettable normality was as bad as the shame of failure. He intended to return famous and heroic or not at all. Unfortunately for his visions of fame and prestige—to say nothing of his continued existence on this mortal coil—it would appear that he would be doing the latter.

He lay in the snow wishing, in this time of weakness and encroaching death that he had instead opted for one the safer, simpler Rites. Anything that would stave off the horrible burning cold, and worse, the encroaching numbness. He had long since lost most of the feeling in his fingers, and he feared frostbite would be setting in soon. Not that it would be an issue for very long. He was lost in the middle of a snowstorm, with no sense of where he was, or from where he’d come. His supplies were exhausted, and his tent had been lost in a moment’s inattention when he’d set it down, and it had rolled into a crevasse hidden by a thin layer of snow. Now as a direct result of his hubris and overconfidence, he was shivering in the meager shelter provided by a snowdrift, laying on his side and hoping for a miracle.

He never actually expected one, which was why it took him a moment to react to the flashing of the white fire in the distance. He blinked twice, before lifting his head up. Clumps of snow fell from his hair and thumped softly into the drift beneath him. When the tiny white speck of illumination failed to disappear as hypothermia-born hallucinations are wont to do, he climbed to his knees. As he did, tendons creaked and bones popped, the deadly chill stiffening them and rendering them unresponsive. The flickering light continued to exist, even as Rake blinked the crusted flakes away from his eyelashes.

He stumbled forward, the fatal frost lining his coat sleeves and pant legs hindering his movements. He fell to his knees, and began crawling. His head dipped, as he lacked the strength to hold it upright. He fell into a rhythm, knowing that if he stopped, he would never start again. Right hand and left knee forward, gasp for breath. Left hand, right knee forward, repeat. Over and over again until finally, his head bumped into something hard and unyielding. With a supreme effort of will, he lifted his head to see what his struggles had brought him before. He was met with a solid, flat wall of white snow. In places, the snow had fallen away to reveal a dull silver, pitted and cracked in places, but still sound. Further above still, was the white fire he had seen before. Except…it wasn’t fire. It blinked steadily, eight feet above Rake’s head. On for a few seconds, then off for another few. Over and over again. The fire neither wavered nor burned, if the snow encrusted around it was any indication. In fact, as Rake squinted more closely, it was contained within a strangely shaped glass bulb.

On. Off.

On. Off.

Rake realized that the pulsing of the white light had him hypnotized, and slowly shook his head. As he did so, his forehead brushed the wall in front of him, and scraped a bit of snow away, revealing more of the dull silver surface. This caused a mini-avalanche, and more ice fell away from the wall in a snaking chain leading up from the base. Once the powder settled, Rake looked up again, and discovered that there was a small square protruding from the wall above him. It jutted out about a quarter-inch from the wall, and was covered with a small metal plate that was gently clacking against the wall in the wind.

Clack . On.

Clack. Off.

Clackclackclack . On.

Clackclack. Off.

Rake hauled himself to his feet by bracing his shoulders against the wall and slowly using his knees, and then his legs to lever himself upward. Once he was in an approximately standing position, he paused for a second, panting, before glancing at the square protrusion.

Clickclack. The cover was still tapping against the wall. Rake shrugged, and lifted it experimentally. The snow caked in between the hinges caused it to jam halfway, but Rake persevered and eventually lifted it to a full ninety degrees from the wall and peered underneath. There was nothing but the shape of a handprint outlined in black glass, crisscrossed with a grid of thin white lines. Rake blinked unsteadily, then pushed his right palm firmly against the handprint, matching his hand’s position to the print’s as best he could.

For a moment, nothing happened. And then, there was a barely perceptible tremble that ran through the wall, and the panel beneath Rake’s hand suddenly warmed. His eyes slowly widened, and by the time he thought to jerk his hand away, the heat had disappeared again. He frowned, and left his hand where it was, savoring the fading remnants of the warmth.

And then, quite suddenly, a vaguely feminine voice whispered with perfectly clarity from just behind his ear, “Access granted.”

Rake jerked, and turned to look behind him. There was nothing there but the endless expanse of blowing white. But when he turned back, he found that an otherwise unremarkable rectangular section of the building in front of him had depressed. Before his eyes, the section of the wall lifted out of sight, leaving a perfectly rectangular doorway leading into dimness. Without waiting to think, Rake stumbled forward into the relative safety of the darkness. The door slammed shut behind him, leaving him in sudden darkness and silence. His breathing was cacophonous in his ears, and just as it occurred to him that this may have been a very bad idea, a single light above him flickered on. Then another. And then in a rush, the entire ceiling became a mess of dim, flickering bulbs like the one he’d seen outside. The room trembled, and he head a distant, muffled whump , followed by a vrrr-um, vrr-um, vrum, vrumvrumvrmvrm, sort of winding up sound that slowly faded into a background humming.

And then the voice spoke again. “Beginning decontamination procedure. Please remain standing.”

Rake narrowly avoiding jumping in surprise this time, but his eyes widened as a translucent wall of white appeared in front of him and began to slowly approach him. Rake backed up slowly, and suddenly found that his back was to the wall he’d entered from. He turned, and tried to find a panel like the one outside that would let him out, but no amount of scrabbling uncovered anything. He looked back and the advancing wall of white was nearly upon him. He curled up against the wall as tight as he could get, and crushed his eyes shut. The humming grew louder and louder, and then it was right on top of him. Any second now the white was going to crush him or vaporize him the way rogue technica did sometimes.

He waited, counting his breaths. One…two…three…four? The humming was growing quieter. He cracked one eye open and found to his surprise that the wall of white was receding. He blinked and looked down at himself. He felt fine. In fact…he felt more than fine. It took him a moment to figure out what was wrong, but when he did, his eyes shot wide open with surprise. All the snow that had been frozen onto his skin and clothing was gone! There was no trace of it. In fact, now that he looked closer, his clothes were cleaner than they’d been in years…

His inspection was interrupted by a wall on the far side of the room opening into a doorway as the previous one had.

“ Decontamination complete. Please proceed,” Came the voice again.

Rake peered around cautiously, waiting for any further apparitions to appear. When none did after several minutes, he started forward, wary of his surroundings. When he passed through the doorway, it shut behind him with an echoing clang. He jumped, but knew better than to look behind him at this point. Ahead of him was a long, rectangular hall. He had never seen anything like it; the walls were perfectly perpendicular to the ground and ceiling. The walls were made of a duller, less-reflective metal than the previous chamber had been, and the ground was a hard, dust-covered, polished stone. Rake’s footsteps echoed between the two perfectly-straight walls, and left footprints in the dust.

As a tiny, tinkling sound came from above, Rake looked up just in time to be blinded by a flash of white fire-light. He gasped, and jerked his head away, rubbing the blindness out of his eyes. He looked up and found whitefires flaring up along the length of the hallway, flickering into a steady, sterile illumination. Rake simply stared for a moment, attempting to comprehend just what it was that he had discovered. This was made more difficult by a number of things, including the increasing pain in his extremities. He grit his teeth as blood flow began to return to his fingers and toes, and his head began to pound. His vision swam and he found himself sitting on the ground, against a wall with no recollection of moving there.

He could feel each individual heart beat throb through his veins, and he squeezed his eyes shut as his head throbbed in sympathy.

Slowly, the pain ebbed, and his senses returned. He drew in a shaking breath and stood up cautiously.

Finally, he looked back the way he’d come. As he expected, there was no sign of the doorway. He turned to look ahead, took a second deep breath and began moving forward. Above his head, the white lights ebbed on and off in sequence, beckoning.

He leaned with one hand trailing against the right wall, seeking with touch as much as sight. He felt a number of bumps and gaps and protrusions and began to recognize what must have been the microscopic gaps between the walls and doors that slid noiselessly out of sight.

His attention was drawn to a dim space ahead of him. The sounds of his footsteps were changing—he was approaching a larger, open area. The radiance of the whitefires above him didn’t seem did little to pierce the gloom.

He stepped out from the hall and hesitantly waited for his eyes to adjust. Judging from the way the sound of his labored breathing was being swallowed by the room, it was even larger than he had initially envisioned.

A long stretch of whitefire hidden behind a shelf near the ceiling weakly illuminated the upper half the room, and brightened the room slightly. Rake’s attention was redirected to a podium directly in front of him as something embedded in it flickered to life. He made his way over to it, and surveyed the area beyond it.

The area beyond the podium was recessed into the floor by a full man-height, and surrounded by a thin, waist-high railing. The bottom of the pit was shrouded in darkness, and seemed to be inhabited by a tangle of strange shapes and dark, stiff-looking ropes.

Rake looked back at the podium that had drawn his attention in the first place. Illuminated in white was a strange shape that, after a moment of staring, Rake realized was a stylized hand with its fingers spread, just like the one outside.

Rake held his hand up, fingers splayed. They were trembling, and still numb from the cold. He looked at the technica panel with its luminous white hand.

He slapped his palm against the panel and shut his eyes tight.

For a moment, nothing happened and his heart sank. Then, a deep, subsonic thump came rolling up through his bones from somewhere beneath him. Then, a rising hum grew around him and he opened his eyes to find that the room was brightening, and more whitefires were flickering into life. For a wild, fleeting instant Rake thought,

Oh spirits, I woke it up and now it’s hungry.

He turned to run, only to find that the doorway he’d come through had been sealed behind him. He dashed to it anyway, and pounded on the blank section of wall where he thought it had been. He looked for a gap, or somewhere he could pry it open but found nothing. Just as he was about to drop to the floor and start searching there, a bloom of radiance behind him made him spin around.

Behind him, suspended above the pit of tangled technica was a transparent figure haloed in white. The brightness obscured any detail, but Rake was certain it was the image of a creature and not just a shape. He could make out a head, but little else. Then, the figure spread its limbs—it’s wings!—and thousands of tiny motes of light exploded away from it, revealing it for what it was.

It was

An eagle, made of shimmering white light, streamers of iridescence pulsing along its wingspan. Its face was inscribed with glowing runes of pale blue, centered on the eyes. And oh, such eyes! They were the gentlest eyes Rake had ever seen—full of weariness and compassion and gentle humor.

And then

The eagle folded her wings back, and fixed those eyes upon Rake’s.

“ Please state your identification,” it said. It spoke in the same voice that Rake had heard when he’d entered the structure, a woman’s voice. Its beak moved in time with its speech, but the words seemed to come from all around.

Rake stared. “W-what?”

The eagle frowned and cocked her head. Her eyes softened. “Your name, please.”

“ Rake, great one,” he stammered.

“ Rake,” she replied. “What is today’s date?”

Was this a test? If so, Rake was well-suited to it—everyone knew the date of the Rite. “January first, great one.”

“ What year, Rake?” She asked, head still cocked.

This flummoxed Rake for a moment. Everyone knew what year it was! Everyone except for eagles made of white light, he supposed… “Two-twenty-six, great one.”

“ Two hundred and twenty six. What happened two hundred and twenty six years ago, Rake?”

“ The Fall, great one. The ancients’ technica failed them.”

The eagle closed her eyes. “Extrapolating,” she said. After a moment, she sighed and opened them again. “Rake, do you know what I am?”

“ A spirit, great one,” he said without hesitation.

She seemed to smile. “And why did you seek me out, Rake?”

“ My Rite, great one. Everyone in the Community needs to do it, to become an adult.”

She closed her eyes, and this time she did smile, the corners of her mouth turning up where it met her beak. “Tomorrow Rake, you will return to them an adult. Today, you may rest here.” A door at the side of the room opened.

Rake bowed low. It seemed appropriate. He turned to leave, and then paused. “Great one? If it pleases you…what is your name?”

The shimmering eagle made of light closed its eyes and smiled once more. “You may call me Snowbird, young Rake.

“ And you are safe now.”
